    Covalent compounds are formed when atoms share electrons. When two electrons are shared between two atoms, this forms a covalent bond. Covalent bonds are quite strong and are difficult to break. Thus covalent compounds remain together as discrete units called molecules, even when dissolved, melted, or boiled.

    How do covalent bonds form and how does sharing electrons help to hold together two atoms? Orbitals are most stable when they have completely filled (or completely empty orbitals). When two atoms come together, their atomic orbitals can combine together to form molecular orbitals. These molecular orbitals are shared between the two atoms and have shapes that resemble the s and p orbitals if they overlap with one another. Importantly, these are still orbitals, and thus molecular orbitals can still only hold 2 electrons.
    ğŸ”—
    Recall from earlier this chapter that the most stable electron configuration for most atoms is 8 valence electrons, because 8 electrons is the number required to completely fill the outer shell s (one of them) and p (three of them) orbitals. This is called the octet rule. So how can we fulfill the octet rule without adding or removing electrons?
    ğŸ”—
    We do this by forming molecular orbitals (which reduces the number of orbitals that have to be filled) and by sharing electrons between the atoms in these molecular orbitals. Consider the molecule \(F_{2}\text{.}\) Each fluorine has 7 valence electrons, for a total of 14 valence electrons. Each fluorine also has a 4 outer shell orbitals, one 2s orbital and three 2p orbitals, for a grand total of 8 outer shell orbitals. If two of those orbitals are combined into one molecular orbital, then the total number of outer shell orbitals is only 7. These 7 orbitals can be filled perfectly by the 14 electrons. Two of these electrons are shared between the two fluorine atoms in the molecular orbital and thus form the bond that links them together. See the figure below:
    ğŸ”—
    The Lewis dot structures of 2 fluorine atoms are shown above. The circles are shown to emphasize the orbitals that the electrons are in. Note: normally a Lewis dot structure does NOT include these circles. In the bottom portion, the two fluorine atoms are joined together by a covalent bond. The red orbital is a molecular orbital that was created by fusing two atomic orbitals together. It contain electrons that came from both of the fluorine atoms.
    ğŸ”—
    Hydrogen is an important common exception to the octet rule. The nearest noble gas to hydrogen is helium, which has a completely filled 1st shell that only requires two electrons. So hydrogen obeys the duet rule for valence electrons; it wants to have two valence electrons rather than 8.

    ğŸ”—
    When it comes to figuring out the structure of larger covalent molecules, the same principles apply (the octet rule, the duet rule, and combining atomic orbitals to form a shared molecular orbital). However, there are additional complexities that also get introduced. For instance, which atoms should be bound to which other atoms? Also, some atoms are capable of forming more than one bond. Carbon, for instance, has 4 valence electrons. That means that it needs 4 other electrons to be shared with it to reach its octet. That means that carbon likes to form 4 covalent bonds!

    Lewis structures: Multiple bonds
    ğŸ”—
    Try to draw the Lewis structure of \(O_{2}\) (or see the figure below). If you follow the rules above, you quickly find that you have a problem, you canâ€™t make an octet around each oxygen atom. How are we supposed to satisfy the octet rule, then?
    ğŸ”—
    We can satisfy the octet rule on both atoms by joining two additional atomic orbitals to form a second molecular orbital and by using this additional molecular orbital to share another pair of electrons. This creates a double bond between the two oxygen atoms. Each bond is a separate molecular orbital that contains a pair of shared electrons.
    ğŸ”—
    In some cases, such as when we try to draw the Lewis structure for \(N_{2}\text{,}\) we need to join still another pair of atomic orbitals to form a third molecular orbital that we can use to share a third pair of electrons. This creates a triple bond.
