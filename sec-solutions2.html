    Molarity
    ðŸ”—
    Chemists often use a different unit of concentration because itâ€™s often helpful to know the number of moles of solute dissolved in solution (because knowing the number of moles is critical if weâ€™re doing any calculations involving chemical reactions). This unit of concentration is molarity. Molarity has a symbol of capital M, which is a shorthand abbreviation for moles/L. Thus a 3.0 M solution of sodium chloride has 3.0 moles of sodium chloride dissolved in 1 L of solution. This can be expressed as the following formula:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} Molarity = \frac{moles\ of\ solute}{L\ of\ solution} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Thus, if 1.5 moles of solute is dissolved in 0.50 L of solution, the molarity is \(\frac{1.5\ moles\ solute}{0.50L\ solution} = 3.0\ M\text{.}\) We can also append the metric prefixes on molarity (ex. mM). The prefix pertains to the number of moles of solute. So mM means \(\frac{millimoles\ solute}{L\ solution}\text{.}\)
