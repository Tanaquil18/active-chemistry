    Measuring equivalents of charge in solution
    ðŸ”—
    In medicine, the concentration of some solutions is measured using units of equivalents (Eq). Equivalents is a way of measuring the amount of charge in a sample of chemical. Equivalents can be calculated as follows:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} Eq = moles \times |charge| \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    So if you have 2 moles of sulfate ions, then you have \(2 \times | - 2| = 2 \times 2 = 4\ Eq\) of sulfate. We can speak about the equivalents of a single ion or about the equivalents from a compound. If we are concerned about an ionic compound, then we calculate the equivalents of charge for each of the ions and then add them together. See the example below:
    ðŸ”—
    Calculate the number of equivalents in 2 moles of potassium sulfate.
    ðŸ”—
    1. Figure out the chemical formula if not provided
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} K_{2}SO_{4} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    1. Figure out the number of moles and charge for each of the ions
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} Charge\ of\ K = + 1 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} Charge\ of\ sulfate = \ - 2 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} moles\ of\ K^{+} = 2\ moles\ K_{2}SO_{4}\left( \frac{2\ moles\ K +}{1\ mole\ K_{2}SO_{4}} \right) = 4\ moles\ K^{+} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} moles\ SO_{4}^{2 +} = 2\ moles\ K_{2}SO_{4}\left( \frac{1\ mole\ SO_{4}^{2 +}}{1\ mole\ K_{2}SO_{4}} \right) = 2\ moles\ SO_{4}^{2 +} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    1. Solve for the number of Eq of both ions then add them together:
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} Eq\ K^{+} = 4\ moles \times |1| = 4\ Eq \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} Eq\ SO_{4}^{2 +} = 2\ moles \times | - 2| = 4\ Eq \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} 4Eq + 4Eq = 8Eq\ total \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    If 5.0g of potassium phosphate is dissolved into 150mL of water, what is the concentration of potassium phosphate in mEq/L.
    ðŸ”—
    1. Figure out the formula for the compound
      ðŸ”—

    \begin{equation*} K_{3}PO_{4} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    1. Convert from grams to moles
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} 5.0g\ K_{3}PO_{4}\left( \frac{mole}{212.265g} \right) = 0.02356\ moles\ K_{3}PO_{4} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    1. Calculate the charge and moles of each ion
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    1. Calculate the Eq of each ion and then add them together

    Dilution calculations
    ðŸ”—
    It is common for solutions to become diluted in science and medicine. Dilution is what happens when more solvent is added to an existing solution. The solute amount remains the same, but the amount of solvent increases, and thus the concentration of the solute in the new solution is lower than it was previously. When dilutions occur, it is helpful to be able to determine what the new concentration of the final solution will be. Likewise, if you are trying to use dilution to prepare a solution with a desired concentration, then you need to know how much of the original solution and solvent you should mix. To aid in these calculations we have the following formula:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} C_{1}V_{1} = C_{2}V_{2} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The Câ€™s are concentration and the Vâ€™s are volume. C1 and V1 represent the concentration of the starting solution and the volume of it that was diluted. C2 and V2 represent the concentration of the final diluted solution and the volume of the final diluted solution. The units on the volumes can be any volume units, as long as the units of V1 and V2 are the same. Likewise, the units on C1 and C2 can be any concentration unit, as long as the units are the same.
